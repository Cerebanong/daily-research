This week's Data Governance landscape was dominated by landmark enforcement actions, new privacy law deadlines, and the formation of a multistate enforcement consortium that signals a more aggressive regulatory posture. Italy's €31.8M fine against Intesa Sanpaolo set a new high watermark for insider threat penalties, while Maryland and Montana privacy laws reached critical enforcement milestones.

## Top Stories

### 1. Italy Fines Intesa Sanpaolo €31.8M Over Systemic Insider Data Breach

Italy's data protection authority imposed a €31.8 million ($36.4M) fine on Intesa Sanpaolo, the country's largest bank, after discovering that a single employee made over 6,600 unauthorized queries accessing 3,573 customers' banking data — including politicians and public figures — over a two-year period without detection. The regulator cited significant weaknesses in monitoring and prevention mechanisms, noting that the operating model allowed operators to query the entire customer base without adequate controls. In a separate case, Intesa was also fined €17.6M for unlawfully processing 2.4 million customers' data transferred to its digital subsidiary Isybank.

**Source:** [The Record](https://therecord.media/italian-regulator-fines-financial-giant-36-million) — Published 2026-03-30
**Why it matters:** The combined €49.4M in fines demonstrates that regulators are increasingly willing to penalize weak insider threat governance, not just external breaches — a critical signal for financial services data leaders.

### 2. Maryland Online Data Privacy Act Reaches Full Enforcement

The Maryland Online Data Privacy Act (MODPA), one of the strictest U.S. state privacy frameworks, became fully enforceable on April 1, 2026. The law prohibits the sale of sensitive personal information, bars data sale and targeted advertising to minors under 18, and imposes strict data minimization requirements. A 60-day cure period applies through April 1, 2027, after which the Maryland AG can bring actions without providing an opportunity to cure.

**Source:** [Verified Credentials](https://news.verifiedcredentials.com/maryland-online-data-privacy-act-enforcement-begins-on-april-1-2026) — Published 2026-04-01
**Why it matters:** Maryland's data minimization and minor protection requirements set a new high bar for U.S. state privacy law, and organizations collecting data from Maryland residents must now comply or face enforcement.

### 3. U.S. State Attorneys General Form Multistate Privacy Enforcement Consortium

U.S. state attorneys general entered into a memorandum of understanding to share expertise and coordinate investigations into privacy violations collectively. This consortium mirrors the multistate data breach enforcement framework of the 2010s, which produced some of the largest settlements in U.S. privacy history.

**Source:** [ArentFox Schiff](https://www.afslaw.com/perspectives/privacy-counsel/new-era-us-privacy-enforcement-has-only-just-begun-2025-trends-and) — Published 2026-04-01
**Why it matters:** Coordinated multistate enforcement dramatically increases the risk and cost of non-compliance, as a single violation can now trigger investigations across multiple jurisdictions simultaneously.

### 4. GDPR Procedural Regulation Enters Into Force, Harmonizing Cross-Border Enforcement

The GDPR Procedural Regulation took effect on January 1, 2026, addressing longstanding frustrations with slow cross-border enforcement. The regulation harmonizes complaint admissibility conditions, introduces a simplified cooperation procedure for straightforward cases, and establishes a 15-month resolution timeframe — aiming to resolve the enforcement bottlenecks that allowed cases to languish for years.

**Source:** [IAPP](https://iapp.org/news/a/digital-governance-in-2026-entropy-and-regulatory-complexity) — Published 2026-04-01
**Why it matters:** Faster cross-border enforcement means organizations can no longer rely on regulatory delays as a de facto compliance buffer — violations will be adjudicated more quickly and consistently across EU member states.

### 5. DORA First Mandatory Reporting Cycle Hits March 2026 Deadline

European financial entities faced the first mandatory Register of Information reporting deadline under the Digital Operational Resilience Act (DORA), requiring them to map every digital vendor in their supply chain using xBRL-CSV format. Penalties for non-compliance reach up to 2% of global turnover, making this one of the most consequential third-party risk reporting mandates in financial services history.

**Source:** [Kiteworks](https://www.kiteworks.com/regulatory-compliance/dora-eu-banks-2026-compliance/) — Published 2026-03-28
**Why it matters:** DORA's vendor mapping requirement creates a new standard of supply chain transparency for financial institutions, with direct implications for data governance teams responsible for third-party data flows.
